# Chapter 5: Social, Legal, Ethical and Professional Issues (SLEP)

The BCS Code of Conduct has been reviewed in relation to this project. Where any of the SLEP issues below have been faced, the mitigations applied are described. All content below is specific to the PRISM project. The BCS Code of Conduct is referenced in the chapter overview (BCS, n.d.).

---

## 5.1 Chapter Overview

This chapter discusses the Social, Legal, Ethical and Professional (SLEP) issues relevant to the PRISM project—a human-centred explainable AI (XAI) system for decision support—and how they have been addressed. The project is conducted in accordance with the University of Westminster Research Ethics Committee approval and the **BCS Code of Conduct** (BCS, n.d.), which sets out four principles: (1) **Public Interest**—regard for public health, privacy, security and wellbeing; (2) **Professional Competence and Integrity**—working within one’s competence, complying with legislation, and avoiding false or negligent action; (3) **Duty to Relevant Authority**—exercising due care, avoiding conflicts of interest, and preserving confidentiality; and (4) **Professional Ambassadorship**—upholding standards and sharing knowledge appropriately.

PRISM does not involve human participants: no interviews, questionnaires, surveys or focus groups are conducted. The research is based on a systematic literature review (SLR) and the design, implementation and technical evaluation of an artefact (revised PPRS; Ethical Form). Therefore, issues such as written consent for interviewees or participant data protection do not arise in the same way as in studies that collect primary data from people. The main SLEP considerations for PRISM are: the **social** impact of explainable AI on trust and accountability; **legal** obligations around data protection and use of public datasets; **ethical** use of AI, transparency of explanations, and adherence to the approved ethical application; and **professional** conduct in line with the BCS Code and institutional requirements. The following section summarises the specific issues identified and the mitigations applied.

## 5.2 SLEP Issues and Mitigation

Summarise the Social, Legal, Ethical and Professional issues in a 2×2 table as below.

| **Social** | **Legal** |
|------------|-----------|
| **Issue:** Explainable AI can affect how non-experts trust or over-trust automated decisions. Poor or misleading explanations may increase automation bias or reduce appropriate reliance on AI in high-stakes domains (e.g. credit, healthcare). PRISM is intended for decision-support contexts where accountability matters. **Mitigation:** (1) Design follows literature-based principles for human-centred XAI (plain-language explanations, SHAP and counterfactuals, clarity of confidence and uncertainty). (2) Export of decision rationales (CSV/PDF) supports auditability so users can justify outcomes. (3) No human-participant studies were conducted; design and evaluation are grounded in the SLR and technical validation, avoiding risks to participants while still aiming for socially beneficial interpretability. | **Issue:** Use of data—including optional user-uploaded CSV—may involve personal or sensitive data. PRISM stores metadata and a limited sample of uploaded rows (e.g. for recent uploads) on the server. Public catalog datasets (e.g. UCI) are used in accordance with their licences. **Mitigation:** (1) Only publicly available, anonymised datasets are used for training and demonstration in the catalog; sources and terms are documented (e.g. UCI Machine Learning Repository). (2) For uploads: file size and row limits are enforced (config: e.g. max 50 MB, 50,000 rows); only metadata and a bounded sample of rows are persisted for recent-upload convenience; users can clear recent uploads. (3) No unnecessary retention of full uploads beyond session/recent list; audit logs record only operational metadata (e.g. filename, row count), not row content. (4) Responsibility for lawful basis and data subject rights for any personal data in uploads rests with the user; the system does not process personal data for purposes beyond the user’s own analysis. |
| **Ethical** | **Professional** |
| **Issue:** (1) Transparency and non-deception: explanations must not mislead users about model behaviour or certainty. (2) Research ethics: the project must align with the approved ethical form (no participants, no intrusive procedures). (3) Fairness and bias: models trained on public datasets may reflect historical biases; explanations should support scrutiny rather than hide limitations. **Mitigation:** (1) SHAP and counterfactual outputs are validated for consistency; confidence and stability/uncertainty are surfaced where applicable so users are not presented as receiving deterministic “facts.” (2) The approved ethical application (no human participants, SLR and technical work only) has been followed throughout; no questionnaires, interviews or user studies were introduced. (3) Bias is addressed in scope as descriptive commentary where relevant (e.g. in explanations or documentation), with no claim that PRISM performs formal fairness auditing; design prioritises transparency so users can question outcomes. (4) Sensitive domains (e.g. credit, health) use only established public datasets and clear labelling of the system as decision-support, not replacement for human judgment. | **Issue:** (1) Working within competence and not overstating capabilities. (2) Reproducibility and integrity of implementation and evaluation. (3) Duty to supervisor and institution (ethics, deadlines, accurate reporting). **Mitigation:** (1) Scope is clearly defined (tabular XAI, SHAP, counterfactuals, technical and literature-based evaluation); no claims beyond what the artefact and SLR support. (2) Codebase is version-controlled; training uses fixed random seed where applicable; public datasets and preprocessing are documented so results can be reproduced. (3) Ethical approval was obtained and adhered to; supervisor feedback and institutional requirements guide reporting; BCS Code principles (competence, integrity, duty to authority) are applied in development and documentation. |

## 5.3 Chapter Summary

This chapter outlined the main Social, Legal, Ethical and Professional issues relevant to PRISM and how they were mitigated. **Social** considerations centred on the impact of explainable AI on trust and accountability; mitigations include literature-based human-centred design, auditability via export, and evaluation without human participants. **Legal** considerations focused on data protection and lawful use of data; mitigations include use of public anonymised catalog datasets, limited retention and minimal logging for uploads, and clear user responsibility for any personal data in uploaded files. **Ethical** considerations covered transparency of explanations, compliance with the approved ethical form (no participants, no questionnaires or interviews), and appropriate handling of bias and sensitivity in high-stakes domains. **Professional** considerations aligned with the BCS Code of Conduct: working within competence, ensuring reproducibility and integrity, and fulfilling duty to supervisor and institution. Together, these measures ensure that PRISM is developed and described in a manner consistent with the BCS Code of Conduct, the approved ethical application, and the revised project proposal (no requirement for questionnaires or ethical approval for human participant studies).
